Logistics Strategy & Performance

Mastering the "7 Rights" through the lens of economic utility and total cost.

1. The 7 Rights (The Logistics Mix)

Every logistics operation aims for the "Perfect Order" by achieving these seven variables simultaneously:

The Basics

  • Right Product: Accuracy in SKUs.
  • Right Quantity: No shortages/overages.
  • Right Condition: Damage-free arrival.

The Execution

  • Right Place: Precise delivery point.
  • Right Time: Avoiding late/early penalties.
  • Right Customer: Authorized receipt.

The Strategy

  • Right Price: Maintaining profitability while achieving the other six rights.

2. The Economic Utility of Logistics

Manufacturing creates Form Utility, but logistics adds value through three distinct economic powers:

Place Utility

Moving products from surplus areas (factories) to deficit areas (retailers).

Time Utility

Ensuring goods are available during peak demand periods (e.g., umbrellas during rain).

Possession Utility

Facilitating the transfer of ownership through reliable delivery networks.

3. Measuring the "Perfect Order"

Individual department metrics often lie. We use a multiplicative formula to find the truth:

The Perfect Order Index (POI) On-Time % × Complete % × Damage-Free % × Accurate Docs %
Case Study: If each metric is 90% (seemingly an 'A' grade), the total POI is only 65.6%. This reveals that nearly 35% of your customers are experiencing a failure.

4. The Total Cost Concept (Trade-offs)

Decisions in logistics are never isolated. Reducing cost in one area often increases it in another.

Action Transport Cost Inventory Cost Strategic Logic
Switch to Air Freight 📈 High 📉 Low Best for high-value/perishable items.
Consolidate Warehouses 📈 High 📉 Low Reduces overhead but increases "Last Mile" distance.
Increase Order Size 📉 Low 📈 High Lower freight rates but higher storage costs.
